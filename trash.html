To engage with the Indian context afresh is to resist the inertia of established interpretations—a task that demands both moral courage and a radical rethinking of inherited ideas. Durkheim’s insights remind us that social facts, those peculiar entities of collective life, must be studied in their own right rather than merely as echoes of universal abstractions. It is only through this rigorous reexamination that we can hope to comprehend the complex tapestry of Indian society, whose vibrant plurality defies reduction to any singular doctrine. In this pursuit, our duty is not only to observe but also to interpret, lest we fall prey to the seductions of the familiar and the inert.
